Problem
The instructions on a $350$ -gram bag of coffee beans say that proper brewing of a large mug of pour-over coffee requires $20$ grams of coffee beans. What is the greatest number of properly brewed large mugs of coffee that can be made from the coffee beans in that bag?
$\textbf{(A) } 16 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 17 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 18 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 19 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 20$
Solution 1
$\frac{350}{20}$ is $17.5$ . We round down to find the maximum he can make (he cannot brew half of a cup), so the answer is $\boxed{\text{(B) }17}$ .

Solution 2
We can use $340$ -grams if we use $17$ mugs. This is because $20 \cdot 17 = 340$ . We cannot use $18$ because that would mean using $360$ -grams. The answer is $\boxed{17}$ .
Note that you can also find this by finding $\lfloor \frac{350}{20} \rfloor = 17.$
~lprado
Solution 3
Dividing $350$ by $20$ , we get $17$ with a remainder of $10$ . However, $10$ grams cannot make a properly brewed large mug of coffee, as stated in the problem. We can only count fully brewed mugs of coffee, hence the answer is $\boxed{\text{(B) }17}$ .

Solution 5 - Cheese (sort of)
We multiply $20$ by each of the answer choices to see if we can find which one is closest and under $350.$ First, we try solution $\text{A}$ , which is $16$ . $16 \cdot 20 = 320,$ which is $30$ off from 350, so $\text{A}$ cannot be the answer, because we can add another $20$ . Moving on to answer choice $\text{B},$ $17 \cdot 20 = 340.$ We notice that $350-340=10,$ and so we cannot add another $20$ to get closer but less than $350.$ (The next number, $18$ , would result in $20 \cdot 18=360,$ which is more than $350$ .) So, the answer is $\boxed{\text{(B) }17}.$

Solution 5: Easiest Way, 1-second Solution
We begin by contemplating the very nature of coffee itself. What is coffee? Is it a beverage? A ritual? A personality? We cast all this aside and instead treat it as a mass, a mere physical quantity subject to the cold brutality of arithmetic. Let the mass of the bag of coffee beans be denoted by the illustrious variable , which the manufacturer, with unspeakable authority, asserts to be exactly grams. Let each mug require a mass of beans we shall call , measured to be grams per mug. Immediately, one might be tempted—rashly, foolishly—to compute and call it a day. But no. We choose suffering.
We now introduce an auxiliary parameter , which plays absolutely no role but makes the whole process look intimidating. Let . We will never use this, but its presence will haunt the rest of the computation.
To pretend this is a calculus problem, we define the Coffee Mug Production Function (CMPF), . You may immediately notice that , which ruins the entire contraption because it simplifies our grandiose function back down to . This is infuriatingly elegant, so we ignore the simplification and proceed as if it doesn’t exist.
We now take the integral of from to , because why not integrate something that doesn't need integrating?
\[\int_0^\pi C(x) \, dx = \int_0^\pi \left( \frac{350}{20} \sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) \right) \, dx\]
We compute the integral term by term.
First, recall the well-known fact that:
\[\int_0^\pi \sin^2(x) \, dx = \int_0^\pi \cos^2(x) \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}\]
Now substitute:
\[\int_0^\pi \left( \frac{350}{20} \sin^2(x) \right) \, dx = \frac{350}{20} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\]
\[\int_0^\pi \cos^2(x) \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}\]
Add them:
\[\frac{350}{20} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} + \frac{\pi}{2} = \left( \frac{350}{20} + 1 \right) \frac{\pi}{2} = (17.5 + 1) \frac{\pi}{2} = 18.5 \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} = 9.25 \pi\]
We step back to admire this: the integral yields . This number has absolutely no bearing on the number of mugs you can make, so we very casually discard this entire page of calculation and return to the original problem.
We now do the actual computation algebraically, but in the most agonizing manner. Express every number in prime factorization:
\[350 = 2 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7, \quad 20 = 2^2 \cdot 5\]
Construct the ratio:
\[\frac{350}{20} = \frac{2 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7}{2^2 \cdot 5}\]
Cancel one factor of 5, cancel a 2, then stare meaningfully at the remaining factors as though performing high magic:
\[= \frac{5 \cdot 7}{2} = \frac{35}{2}\]
But wait—this looks like 17.5, not 35. Alarm bells ring. This means at some point we made a cancellation that was mathematically legal but dramatically inconvenient. Realizing the audience expects whole mugs, we backtrack, undo the cancellations, and instead try division the old-fashioned way.
Divide 350 by 20:
20 goes into 35 one (1) time, leaves remainder 15. Bring down 0 → 150.
20 goes into 150 seven (7) times, leaves remainder 10.
20 goes into 10 zero (0) times, but that fractional mug would be a crime against baristas everywhere, so we stop.
Thus, the number of whole mugs is 17?
No.
That was long division ignoring the prior error. Banish it.
Finally, returning to sanity:
\[\frac{350}{20} = 17.5 \text{ mugs worth of coffee}\]
but you can only make whole mugs properly.
To maximize the number of properly brewed mugs, we take the floor of 17.5:
\[\lfloor 17.5 \rfloor = 17\]
